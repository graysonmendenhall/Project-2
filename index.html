There are six master soil horizons that are commonly recognized. They are designated using the capital letters O, A, E, B, C, and R and are categorized based on their composition and depth within the soil profile. The master layers are not present in every soil profile.

Digging in the Dirt

Identification and description of the horizons present at a given site is the first step in soil classification. Soil scientists often dig a large hole, called a soil pit, usually several meters deep and about a meter wide, to expose soil horizons for study.

It's All About Layers

A soil horizon is a layer generally parallel to the soil surface, whose physical characteristics differ from the layers above and beneath. Each soil type usually has three or four horizons. Horizons are defined in most cases by obvious physical features, chiefly color and texture.

The 'O' Layer

This layer generally forms above the mineral soil or occurs in an organic soil profile. The "O" stands for organic matter. It is a surface layer dominated by the presence of large amounts of organic material derived from dead plant and/or animal residues which is in varying stages of decomposition.

The 'A' Layer

Often referred to as the 'topsoil,' this layer generally contains enough partially decomposed (humified) organic matter to give the soil a color darker than that of the lower horizons. The A horizons are often coarser in texture, having lost some of the finer materials by translocation to lower horizons and by erosion. This layer is known as the zone in which the most biological activity occurs.

The 'E' Layer

The 'E' layer has been significantly leached of clay, iron, and aluminum oxides, which leaves a concentration of resistant minerals, such as quartz, in the sand and silt sizes. These are present only in older, well-developed soils, and are generally lighter in color than either the horizon above or below it. E horizons are commonly found in soils developed under forests.

The 'B' Layer

B horizons form below an O, A, or E horizon and they have undergone sufficient changes during soil genesis, such that the properties of their original parent material are no longer discernible. The B horizon is commonly referred to as the "subsoil". In humid regions, B horizons are the layers of maximum accumulation of materials such as silicate clays, iron, aluminum oxides, and organic material.

The 'C' Layer

The C horizon forms as the R horizon beneath it weathers and rocks break up into smaller particles. While loose enough to be dug with a shovel, C horizon material often retains some of the structural features of the parent rock or geologic deposits from which it formed.

The 'R' Layer

R horizons are the layer of partially weathered bedrock at the base of the soil profile. Unlike the above layers, R horizons are composed largely of consolidated masses of hard rock that cannot be excavated by hand.
